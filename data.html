Price vs Sentiment Correlation Chart   ↓

Correlation between sentiment and price movements.

Insights:
1. No Consistent Lead-Lag Relationship
   → Sometimes sentiment increases before a price rise (e.g., late May), suggesting possible predictive power.
   → But in many other cases, price and sentiment diverge, or sentiment is reactive (i.e., it shifts after price moves).

2. High Volatility in Sentiment
   → Sentiment spikes up and down frequently, even when price is relatively flat (e.g., July 2021). This suggests Reddit sentiment is noisy and possibly influenced by non-price-related factors like memes, hype, or unrelated market chatter.

3. General Trend Decoupling
   → From April to August, average sentiment remains mostly elevated, while the stock price moves sideways to slightly down.
   → This decoupling suggests that high average sentiment doesn't always translate into upward price momentum.

Sentiment Momentum Analysis Chart   ↓

Analysis of sentiment momentum and its impact on price.

Insights:
Positive Correlation Trend (Weak but Present):
   → As sentiment increases toward the right (more positive), there’s a slight increase in the concentration of green dots (positive returns).
   → Conversely, more red dots cluster near the left (negative sentiment), especially around and below 0.

Most Negative Returns Cluster Near Neutral Sentiment:
   → Many price drops occur even at mild-to-moderate sentiment levels (0.10–0.25).
   → This suggests sentiment might not always provide enough momentum or signal strength on its own to predict up or down moves.

Outliers:
   → There's a massive outlier at ~0.18 sentiment and +20% next-day return — likely an unusual spike driven by a major event.
   → These rare extremes skew visual interpretation but highlight that sentiment spikes can occasionally align with major moves.

Sentiment Lag Analysis Chart   ↓

Analysis of delayed price reactions to sentiment changes.

Insights:
Volatility in Sentiment
   → Sentiment is highly volatile, with frequent sharp swings day-to-day.
   → This volatility often does not translate into corresponding price shifts, weakening its predictive power.

Possible Local Correlations
   → In some time windows (e.g., early March, mid-May), a drop in sentiment seems to precede or accompany price declines, but these are infrequent.
   → You’d need statistical lag correlation tests (e.g., cross-correlation functions) to determine whether any meaningful lag exists.

Trading Volume vs Reddit Comments Chart   ↓

Relationship between trading volume and Reddit comment activity.

Insights:
Spike Alignment
   → There are multiple points where comment spikes align with volume surges:
   → Most notably in late January, early March, and early August, both trading volume and Reddit comments spike simultaneously.
   → This implies Reddit hype may amplify market participation, likely from retail traders.

Asymmetry in Influence
   → Some volume spikes occur without a Reddit comment spike, suggesting other factors (e.g., earnings reports, macro events) also drive trading volume.
   → But every major comment spike seems to coincide with higher volume, which supports the idea that Reddit chatter contributes to increased trading activity, especially on the retail side.

Lag Not Obvious
   → There’s no clear lag (e.g., Reddit comments leading volume by a day or two). Peaks tend to happen concurrently.

Volume vs Extreme Daily Sentiment Chart   ↓

Analysis of trading volume in relation to extreme daily sentiment.

Insights:
Some Co-movement in Peaks
   → Several high sentiment intensity days (red peaks above 0.7–0.8) occur alongside or just before volume surges (e.g., early February, mid-March, late April).
   → Suggests that emotionally charged days, whether bullish or bearish, may draw more trader attention

Diminishing Volume Over Time
   → While extreme sentiment remains relatively elevated and volatile throughout, trading volume trends downward across the timeline.
   → This implies that emotional intensity remains, but market participation fades — possibly due to fading hype or exhaustion in retail activity.

Divergence in Mid–Late Summer
   → From June to August, many high-sentiment days don’t correspond with volume spikes, breaking the earlier pattern.
   → Suggests that sentiment alone isn't enough to sustain volume; the novelty or urgency of the narrative may matter more.
